Petworth House

Petworth House, a Grade I listed mansion, is the centrepiece of this remarkable estate. Built in the late 17th century, with later additions in the 18th and 19th centuries, it epitomizes the grandeur of English country estates. The house has been home to the Percy family, the Earls of Northumberland, for over 900 years, and its walls resonate with stories of aristocratic life, political intrigue, and cultural patronage. Visitors to Petworth House are treated to a rich tapestry of architectural styles and period furnishings. From the opulent Baroque staterooms to the intimate family quarters, each chamber offers a glimpse into a bygone era.

The house boasts one of the most significant collections of classical and contemporary art in the National Trust’s care. The magnificent Turner paintings, inspired by the picturesque landscapes of the estate, are among the highlights of the collection. Additionally, visitors can marvel at works by other esteemed artists, including William Blake, Thomas Gainsborough, and Sir Joshua Reynolds.

Turner’s connection to the estate is commemorated in the Turner Wing, a dedicated gallery space showcasing his works and celebrating his enduring legacy.

Arundel Castle

Arundel Castle’s story begins nearly a millennium ago, with its construction commencing in the 11th century by Roger de Montgomery, Earl of Arundel, shortly after the Norman Conquest of England. Since then, the castle has witnessed a myriad of historical events, from the turbulence of medieval conflicts to the opulence of the Renaissance and beyond.

Over the centuries, Arundel Castle has been home to many influential families, including the Fitzalans, and the Dukes of Norfolk. Each generation left its mark on the castle, expanding and enhancing its structure and splendour. Today, the castle remains the ancestral home of the Duke of Norfolk, one of the premier noble titles in England.

From its humble beginnings as a Norman stronghold to its current status as a symbol of English heritage, the castle stands as a testament to the enduring spirit of the people who built and preserved it over the centuries.

Weald and downland living museum

The Weald and Downland Living Museum, located in Singleton, near Chichester in West Sussex, England, is an open-air museum that brings to life the history of rural life in the South East of England. The museum spans over 40 acres of the South Downs National Park and features a collection of over 50 historic buildings dating from the 13th to the 19th centuries. These buildings have been carefully dismantled, conserved, and rebuilt on the site to represent various types of rural architecture and to showcase traditional trades and crafts.

Visitors to the Weald and Downland Living Museum can explore a variety of buildings including houses, farm buildings, workshops, and public buildings, each telling the story of the people who lived and worked in them over the centuries. The museum is not only about the buildings themselves but also about the landscapes, the animals that live there, and the gardens and crops that were grown.

The museum has been the filming location for the BBC historical documentary series "The Repair Shop." The show features skilled craftspeople restoring cherished family items and antiques to their former glory, often revealing the personal stories and histories behind the objects.
